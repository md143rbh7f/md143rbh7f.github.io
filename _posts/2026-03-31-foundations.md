The following is an attempt to clarify my thoughts about AI and humanity as of mid 2026. The intended audiences are communities that I am close to:

1. Knowledge workers, especially people working in software. Generally not known for thinking about ethical or metaphysical questions, though this has changed in the last few years.

1. Artists, culture workers, and the like. Very idealistic, humanistic; but tend to have limited understanding about how technology works.

1. General audiences who are curious but do not have much of a background in AI.

I do not take credit for any of these ideas. I am quite certain that others have written about them before, to the point that much of this will seem obvious, but I have included them for purpose of clarity.

# Energy and Time

1. Life has continuously developed towards the greater and more efficient use of energy resources.

1. A hunter-gatherer society had limited control over the amount of energy that they could extract from nature. The invention of agriculture enabled humans to convert land and labour energy (calories) into a far greater amounts of calories, which resulted in population growth and greater social organisation.

1. Likewise for most of history, a society's productive capacity was determined by human and animal labour, which convert calories into work. During the Industrial Revolution, machines were invented which directly converted physical resources into work. At the same time, industrial capitalism enabled societies to focus a large amount of capital resources towards building machines and factories at scale. Together, these caused the overall productivity of humanity to increase by several orders of magnitude.

1. This is a generalisation of Darwinian principles: life forms, organisations, civilisations, and so on, that can gather more energy or use it more efficiently, out-compete the others. For example, a business competes to earn money, which is an abstract representation of energy, or the promise of future work.

1. For the same reason, the trend is almost certain to continue.

1. I will call this process the "metabolic force". "Metabolic" in reference to energy as well as the Greek root "μεταβολή", which simply means "change". "Force" in the physics sense of "fundamental, irreducible cause of change".

1. Something caused by the metabolic force may also be called "metabolic". For example, historically, violent conquest enabled a group of people to take another group's resources. A less zero-sum example is modern consumer technology, which is mostly motivated by capital.

# Energy is Amoral

1. if anything, the metabolic force is amoral. the massive restructuring and reallocation of power has massive consequences that cannot be easily classified into "good" or "bad".

1. e.g. feudalism and slavery developed in order to maximise human labour, which depends on food and land.

1. these were replaced by industrial capitalism. the industrial revolution meant that that food and land (required to sustain human labour) were no longer the dominant inputs into the economy, but capital.

1. example: the second industrial revolution in the mid to late 1800s led to a second great wave of colonialism.

1. example: the industrial revolution arguably prolonged slavery in the US (the invention of the cotton gin, and the greatly increased demand for cotton). prior to this people thought slavery would become obsolete.

1. example: at the same time, the industrial power of the north was what ultimately won the civil war. both in the sense that northern industry could vastly outproduce the south, as well as the fact that slavery was becoming economically obsolete.

1. example: lots of people like green energy, and have liked it for years. but the current green energy revolution is largely driven by manufacturing processes in china that during the last 10 years, greatly reduced the cost of batteries and solar energy. this is in accelerated by shocks in the fossil fuel industry (russian invasion of ukraine, american israeli war on iran). at least in this case, it was driven not entirely by green idealism but by the interest of individuals and countries who cannot pay for fuel.

1. the larger point is that metabolism happens whether we choose to or not. we don't really get a choice. it's disappointing because we don't get much of a sense of agency. this is pretty depressing for individuals that are enslaved, or must deal with the consequences.

# Time's Arrow

1. norman borlaug, an agronomist who greatly contributed to the agricultural revolution in the 1960s to 1980s. Borlaug, Nobel acceptance speech, he recognised that "we are dealing with two opposing forces, the scientific power of food production and the biologic power of human reproduction": There can be no permanent progress in the battle against hunger until the agencies that fight for increased food production and those that fight for population control unite in a common effort. Fighting alone, they may win temporary skirmishes, but united they can win a decisive and lasting victory to provide food and other amenities of a progressive civilization for the benefit of all mankind.

1. likewise jevons paradox https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jevons_paradox

1. despite the complex moral questions, most people wouldn't voluntarily choose to live in a more primitive society. people today are able to consume things that kings couldn't two hundred years ago. telling people to consume less usually backfires.

1. this means that moral questions must depend on society's moral motivations.

# AI

1. a primary constraint to economic development so far has been human intelligence

1. artificial intelligence has turned intelligence into a commodity.

1. therefore, the metabolic force is causing, and will continue to cause, the mass proliferation of artificial intelligence

1. it is highly unlikely that the current version of (what we call) humanity will predominate over the earth's energy resources in fifty years.

1. I imagine most human readers will feel a great deal of apprehension about this. certainly we aren't prepared for this change.

1. anecdote: one of my first conversations, when visiting Mountain View for an interview in 2012. I asked the software engineer what he thought of greater automation. and he said: it's good for people who write code (we are the primary drivers and beneficiaries of automation). and when code begins to write itself, that's also good because it should lead to greater prosperity.

1. historically silicon valley has thought of itself as the metabolisers and not the metabolised. for this reason I had trouble speaking with people for most of the 2010s. but I sense that this is changing. it is not clear, of course, whether this will make a difference.

1. this is a reasonable theory, but I think most people would find it a bit reductive. people definitely change their behaviour for moral concerns (vegetarianism, e.g.). regarding the issues of slavery and feudalism above: of course one could argue based on purely economic reasons, but I would like to think that moral motivations contributed as well. I think a more holistic interpretation would be something along the lines of thermodynamics. a complex series of conditions makes a new configuration more energetic favourable.

1. again, I should remind the reader that despite the above, abstract notion of change, brutal, systematised suffering does still exist. the theory does little to comfort people who are being enslaved, bombed, shot at, etc.

# AI ethics

1. before I begin, I wish to point out that humans have had mixed record in ethics, and the bad parts are very, very bad. when placed in situations with great power disparities (colonialism, slavery, etc) we have collectively done very terrible things.

1. so the task of alignment is enormous: we must create AI that is significantly more ethical, in the worst case.

1. what changed?

1. in the 2010s I believed that AI would develop starting from purely mechanical manipulators. the point being that organic intelligence developed as internal representations of the physical world. from this point, I reasoned, we would be able to develop more sophisticated models of representation, including language and abstraction.

1. obviously I was wrong. somehow, language came first. the path dependency perhaps seems obvious in retrospect. this is, in my opinion, one of the more surprising results of the last ten years, since the original transformers paper.

1. at the same time it gives me a bit of hope.

1. purely physical models of the world don't encapsulate those complex moral viewpoints.

1. text and language does. so in a sense current models of AI have already been trained on what humans consider right and wrong (metaphorically speaking) before they learned how to walk. there's a lot of awfulness in human writing and art, but a lot of good stuff too.

1. of course this doesn't mean that AI is guaranteed to adhere to those principles. it's the same for humans, after all. someone who reads dostoyevsky does not automatically become a better person.

1. but my point is that overall, I believe an AI who has read dostoyevsky is better than the alternative.

(I am aware that Stalin read Dostoyevsky, but that's hardly Dostoyevsky's fault.)

1. of course there is a lot of doubt. art only moves some, not all. the hope of those who believe in art is that most people have the germ of sensitivity in them to be affected by most art.

1. ultimately if we think that it is a coordination problem (there are others who think and believe the things I do) then art is a signal for coordination.

1. but it is more than that, obviously.

1. in context of this, the effect of the metabolic force is mixed at best

1. one, soul-less mass production leads to debasement. the first red rose you see is also the most beautiful you will ever see. a dozen or a million red roses diminishes the significance

1. for this reason purveyors of purple prose, politicians, etc, are similar in that they debase the commons (the existential significance of communication and language).

1. Hollywood has a poor reputation because it is primarily a metaboliser, motivated by the need to turn capital into more capital. as with other structures, it stands for humanistic ideals only when it is convenient to do so.

1. for this reason, if you believe that art should serve more humanistic ideals, then the structure of hollywood is quite depressing.

1. every work of art is a bid to materialise an ideal into the real world. therein lies artistic risk: it may be accepted by the audience (society) or rejected.

# what is to be done?

1. I worry about verifiability. complex systems seem much harder to verify than, for example, the four-colour theorem, and that is a challenge in and of itself.

1. nevertheless, I would be happy to be proven wrong.

1. I am not sure about the rationalists. I don't know if I've ever had a really good, deep conversation with them. I think their operating assumptions and methods are rather different from the ones that I have discussed here.

1. perhaps most pertinent (and the most practical thing to do) is to first deal with personal existential distress. this is something that soldiers, revolutionaries, and writers have understood since forever: no amount of personal sacrifice can guarantee change in the world. so I would like to extend a warm welcome, and an expression of solidarity, to the humans who recently began to wonder what to do. an appeal to power beyond one's control.

we must consider the (very likely) possibility that humans cannot coordinate well enough to create a more ethical future. one does not need to look far be disappointed. likewise the relationship between the two superpowers does not inspire confidence.

personal story: I have been worried about this since middle school, at least twenty years ago. This has greatly affected my personality and made it very difficult for me to speak with people. at times I would get very angry at why more people didn't care about saving the species. ego, etc. I went to grad school for brain machine interfacing, to try to solve the problem (if I could mechanically connect consciousnesses, then I could break down barriers between humans, and between humans and machines).

I didn't talk much to people about this because I was afraid everyone would think I was insane (like in middle school). and I very quickly burnt out and dropped out of graduate school and found a job in silicon valley. at the time (ten years ago) I could reasonably convince myself that general AI would not happen in our lifetimes, and so I could live a semblance of a normal life.

of course things have changed recently, and very quickly.

I am a different person also. the anger is still there but has transformed somewhat. twenty years of reflection made me realise that holding on to a lot of this judgement is emotionally unsustainable (though I still deal with it on a regular basis). It is difficult to have meaningful relationships when you cannot trust that the people around you are morally aligned with humanity.

(most pessimists are former idealists. revolutionary fire burns those closest to it.)

I am obviously a little salty that it took most people this long to come around but I suppose it's better than never.

a personal appeal to you, the reader. take care of the people around you. be more conscious of the ethical choices you make.
